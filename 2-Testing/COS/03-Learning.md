# Learning: Closing the Feedback Loop

## Quick Answer

**Learning closes the feedback loop by systematically capturing evidence after each action, then using that evidence to update and strengthen your Knowledge Base.** Without deliberate learning, each execution feels isolated. With it, patterns compound and decisions improve continuously. The loop never closes by accident—it requires specific disciplines: capture evidence immediately, review weekly, promote patterns only after repetition, and actively search for contrary evidence to avoid bias.

---

## The Loop

```
Knowledge Base (what you know)
    ↓ informs
Execution (what you do)
    ↓ produces
Evidence (task → outcome → surprise)
    ↓ updates
Knowledge Base (loop repeats, stronger)
```

**Summary:** Learning is the evidence-update side of the loop. Most people execute and move on. The system learns because you intentionally close the loop by capturing, reviewing, and promoting evidence into lasting patterns.

---

## Part 1: What Counts as Evidence?

**Summary:** Not all occurrences are evidence. Evidence requires three elements: a specific observation (what you saw), context (who, when, constraints), and outcome (what happened as a result). Vague observations without outcome or context add noise to your Knowledge Base and slow learning.

### **The Three Required Elements**

Evidence = **Observation + Context + Outcome**

**Example:**
- **Observation:** Candidate didn't respond to 3 emails over 1 week
- **Context:** Cold outreach, no prior relationship, mid-budget role
- **Outcome:** Moved to next candidate (lost pipeline slot)
- **Evidence learned:** Cold outreach effectiveness varies by relationship type

### **Good Evidence vs Bad Evidence**

| Good Evidence | Bad Evidence |
|---|---|
| Cold email got 0% response (specific) | "It didn't work" (too vague) |
| 5 candidates, no response in 7 days (repeatable) | "The person was busy" (context without outcome) |
| Lead scored 2/10, rejected in screening (measurable) | "I don't know why it failed" (observation without learning) |
| Escalated with call after 48h silence, got response (actionable) | "Things went well" (no specificity) |

**Critical rule:** Every evidence capture must pass all three tests (observation, context, outcome) or it doesn't count.

## Part 2: The Five Evidence Questions

**Summary:** After every significant action, systematically answer five questions about what happened. These questions move you from "something occurred" to "I understand what to do next time." Each question builds on the previous one: expectation vs reality, reason for divergence, pattern vs anomaly, root cause, and next action. Without these questions, evidence stays scattered and patterns never emerge.

### **Question 1: Did my expectation match reality?**

| Element | Example |
|---|---|
| Expected | Application would pass screening (based on Knowledge Base) |
| Actual | Rejected in 24 hours |
| Match | NO |
| Evidence learned | Application approach is either wrong or not suitable for this company type |

### **Question 2: If not, why did it diverge?**

**List all plausible reasons:**
- Misunderstood the requirement (they wanted X, I emphasized Y)
- Background doesn't fit (worse than expected)
- Application strategy was weak
- Company was a bad fit
- Timing was wrong

**Action:** Rank by likelihood and choose the most probable reason to investigate.

### **Question 3: Is this a one-time anomaly or a pattern?**

**Track data points:**
- Application 1 (similar role, different company): PASSED screening
- Application 2 (same company type, different role): REJECTED screening
- Application 3 (similar to #1): PASSED screening

**Result:** Pattern emerging—passes when role-specific, fails when background-focused. Not an anomaly.

### **Question 4: If pattern, what's the underlying cause?**

| Element | Example |
|---|---|
| Hypothesis | Screening is role-specific, not background-specific |
| Supporting evidence | When I emphasize role fit, pass rate improves |
| Contradicting evidence | None yet |
| Confidence tier | GUESSED (3 data points, could still be luck) |

### **Question 5: What should I do differently next time?**

**Define specific changes:**
- Always lead with role-fit evidence, not background
- Tailor application to specific role, not generic pitch
- Test with next 5 applications

**Define success metric:**
- If pass rate moves from 33% to 60%+, promote confidence tier to KNOWN

## Part 3: Promoting Evidence to Knowledge

**Summary:** Not all evidence becomes permanent knowledge. Evidence gets promoted only when it meets four strict rules: sufficient repetition, survival across different contexts, consistent action (the mitigation works reliably), and a system for tracking contrary evidence. Premature promotion causes false confidence; delayed promotion prevents learning. Use explicit thresholds for each rule.

### **Rule 1: Repetition Required**

**Promotion thresholds by stakes:**

| Stakes Level | Instances Needed | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Low (learning a preference) | 2-3 instances | Client prefers phone calls |
| Medium (learning a pattern) | 5-10 instances | Cold email response rate |
| High (acting on it regularly) | 20+ instances | Critical failure mitigation |

**Critical rule:** Never promote one success to pattern. One anomaly teaches nothing.

### **Rule 2: Survival Under Variation**

**Pattern must hold across different contexts:**

| Test | Condition | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Test 1 | Cold email to recruiter | No response |
| Test 2 | Cold email to hiring manager | No response |
| Test 3 | Cold email to peer | No response |
| Test 4 | Cold email with referral warm-up | GOT response |

**Revised pattern:** "Cold email works when preceded by warm introduction; fails cold."
**Confidence:** KNOWN (4 tests across contexts, pattern holds)

### **Rule 3: Consistency of Action**

**Pattern is real only if the mitigation reliably works:**

| Test | Action | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Test 1 | Called after email silence | Candidate responded with explanation ✓ |
| Test 2 | Called, got voicemail, sent text | Candidate responded ✓ |
| Test 3 | Called, got voicemail, waited 1 week | No response ✗ |
| Test 4 | Called within 48h of interest | Candidate confirmed ✓ |

**Refined pattern:** "Mitigation works best within 48 hours; success ~75%."
**Confidence:** KNOWN (4 tests, time window matters)

### **Rule 4: Marker for Contrary Evidence**

**Keep watching for exceptions:**
- **Known pattern:** "Cold email works when warm-introduced"
- **Exception found:** Cold email worked without intro (1 instance)
- **Action:** Don't abandon pattern (1 exception ≠ reversal)
- **Refine:** Mark as "exception with context," watch for more
- **Updated pattern:** "Works 80% with warm intro, 10% cold (rare)"

## Part 4: Confidence Tiers and When They Change

**Summary:** Every pattern in your Knowledge Base carries a confidence tier that tells you how much evidence supports it and how much you can trust it. Tiers change only when data justifies promotion. Promotion follows strict criteria: GUESSED requires 2-3 instances, SUSPECTED requires 3-5 consistent observations, KNOWN requires 10+ instances across contexts, PROVEN requires 100+ with contradictions impossible. Tier accuracy is critical—marking something KNOWN when it's GUESSED breaks the feedback loop.

### **Confidence Tiers and Actions**

| Tier | Definition | Data Needed | How to Act |
|---|---|---|---|
| **GUESSED** | Initial hypothesis | 1-2 instances | Provisional; test before betting on it |
| **SUSPECTED** | Multiple observations | 3-5 consistent observations | Can act on it, but escalate if high stakes |
| **KNOWN** | Observed repeatedly | 10+ instances across contexts | Can act autonomously |
| **PROVEN** | Consistent law of nature | 100+ instances, contradictions impossible | Can hardcode as rule |

### **Promotion Progression**

**Timeline example:**

| Stage | Instances | Tier | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 1-2 | GUESSED | "Cold email might be ineffective" |
| After 5 tries | 5 | SUSPECTED | "Cold email seems ineffective" |
| After 10+ varied | 10+ | KNOWN | "Cold email is ineffective for [this audience]" |
| After 100+ across audiences | 100+ | PROVEN | "Cold email ineffective for [these audiences], effective for [those]" |

**Critical rule:** Promotion is one-way upward. Demotion (from KNOWN to SUSPECTED) happens only when contradicting evidence emerges.

## Part 5: The Learning Discipline

**Summary:** The feedback loop breaks without discipline. Four practices keep it healthy: capture evidence immediately after actions (don't wait), review evidence weekly (spot pattern breaks and tier changes), promote slowly with explicit thresholds (prevent overfitting), and delete false patterns (they corrupt future decisions). These practices compound over time—each week refines your Knowledge Base and makes subsequent decisions better.

### **Practice 1: Capture Evidence Immediately**

**After every significant action, record within 24 hours:**

| Field | Example |
|---|---|
| Date | 2026-04-17 |
| Action | Phone call to recruiter after 3 days of silence |
| Expected (from KB) | Either conversation or voicemail with callback |
| Actual | Caught them, 10-min chat, interested in scheduling interview |
| Surprise? | No surprise, pattern held |
| Tier update | Candidate ghosting mitigation confirmed (call works better than email) |

**Why immediately:** Memory fades. Details become fuzzy. Capture within 24 hours while context is fresh.

### **Practice 2: Review Weekly**

**Every week, ask these questions:**
- Did any patterns break? (Evidence contradicted an assumption)
- Should any tiers change? (Enough repetition to promote/demote)
- Do contradictions exist? (Two patterns conflict)

**Question to ask:** "Is there anything in my Knowledge Base that should shift from GUESSED to KNOWN, or vice versa?"

### **Practice 3: Promote Slowly**

**Make promotion hard. Set explicit criteria before promoting:**
- "Only promote GUESSED → SUSPECTED if I see it 2 more times" (not after first success)
- "Only promote SUSPECTED → KNOWN if it works across 3 different contexts" (not just repeated in same context)

**Why:** This prevents overfitting. You learn real patterns, not noise.

### **Practice 4: Delete When Disproven**

**When evidence decisively contradicts a pattern, act:**

| Step | Example |
|---|---|
| Old pattern | "Candidates prefer email introductions" |
| Contradicting evidence | Called 5 people, all preferred calls or text |
| Action | Delete from "KNOWN patterns" |
| Replacement | "Candidates prefer direct/synchronous contact" |

**Why:** False patterns corrupt future decisions. Better to delete than to let them mislead you.

## Part 6: When Learning Breaks (And How to Fix It)

**Summary:** Four traps disrupt learning: confirmation bias (seeing only confirming evidence), absence-of-evidence fallacy (concluding something doesn't exist because you haven't seen it), insufficient sample size (promoting patterns from 2-3 instances), and correlation-causation confusion (assuming two correlated things are causally linked). Each trap corrupts your Knowledge Base differently. Each has a specific fix. Knowing these traps lets you avoid them.

### **Trap 1: Confirmation Bias**

**What happens:** You only see evidence that confirms what you already believe.

| Element | Example |
|---|---|
| Belief | "Cold email doesn't work" |
| Evidence collected | 3 cold emails with no response ✓ |
| Ignored evidence | 1 cold email that got response (dismissed as "anomaly") |
| Result | Never update to "maybe cold email works sometimes" |

**Fix:** Actively search for contrary evidence. If your belief is "X never works," go find one example where it worked and analyze what was different.

### **Trap 2: Absence of Evidence = Evidence of Absence**

**What happens:** You conclude something doesn't exist because you haven't seen it.

| Element | Example |
|---|---|
| Observation | "Haven't seen any job candidates who X" |
| Faulty conclusion | "X is impossible for candidates" |
| Reality | You haven't looked, or your search is biased |

**Fix:** Don't promote negative beliefs ("this never works") without active search. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

### **Trap 3: Insufficient Sample Size**

**What happens:** You draw conclusions from 2-3 instances when you need 10+.

| Instance | Action | Result | False Conclusion |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cold email | No response | "Cold email doesn't work" |
| 2 | Cold email | No response | "Confirmed, doesn't work" |
| 3+ | (never reached) | — | (decision already made) |

**Fix:** Use explicit thresholds before promoting. "I will not promote this until I have N data points and they vary on context Y."

### **Trap 4: Correlation ≠ Causation**

**What happens:** Two things happen together, so you assume one caused the other.

| Element | Example |
|---|---|
| Observation | When I follow up with a call, candidates respond |
| False assumption | "Calling causes response" |
| Reality | Maybe I call only when already interested, so they were going to respond anyway |

**Fix:** Test the intervention in isolation. Don't observe correlation; test causation with a controlled change.

## Part 7: Compounding Knowledge

**Summary:** The feedback loop's power is compounding: each execution produces evidence, evidence refines your Knowledge Base, better knowledge guides better execution, which produces better evidence. Over six months, a strategy that started at 10% success can reach 60%+ not because you became more qualified, but because you learned systematically and honestly. This is the core mechanism of skill development without upfront expertise.

### **Six-Month Compounding Example**

| Month | Results | Evidence | Action | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 10% pass screening | Strategy needs work | Lead with role fit, not background | Direction set |
| 2 | 25% pass screening | Update helped (KNOWN) | Promote role-fit strategy | Confidence builds |
| 3 | 40% pass screening | Continue refining needed | Investigate timing, company type | Pattern deepens |
| 4 | 50% pass screening | Timing + company type matter | Create variant strategies | Specialized |
| 5 | 55% pass screening | Diminishing returns appearing | Refocus on rare failures | Edge cases |
| 6 | 60%+ pass screening | System is optimized | Maintain and watch for breaks | Sustainable |

### **Why Compounding Happens**

1. **Each execution produces evidence**
2. **Evidence refines Knowledge Base**
3. **Better Knowledge Base guides better execution**
4. **Better execution produces better evidence**
5. **Loop strengthens, acceleration increases**

**Critical insight:** You don't need to be smart upfront. You need to learn fast and honestly. The feedback loop does the rest.

## Part 8: When to Escalate from Learning

**Summary:** Sometimes your evidence tells you to escalate and ask for expert help. Three situations require escalation: evidence contradicts your core domain assumptions (you're missing something fundamental), confidence tier is stuck at GUESSED despite repeated attempts (pattern won't emerge), or stakes are too high to learn safely from failure (costs of mistakes are irreversible). Escalation doesn't mean quitting—it means switching to guided learning instead of solo learning.

### **Escalation Triggers**

**1. Evidence contradicts core assumptions**
- **Sign:** Every approach fails; you're missing something fundamental
- **Example:** "Every application gets rejected; my resume approach is fundamentally wrong"
- **Action:** Get expert perspective; rebuild Knowledge Base from scratch

**2. Confidence tier stuck at GUESSED**
- **Sign:** Tier won't promote despite repeated attempts; pattern won't emerge
- **Example:** "I've tried this 10 ways; can't reproduce the success"
- **Action:** Ask someone who's done this successfully; learn from their Knowledge Base

**3. Stakes too high to learn from failure**
- **Sign:** Mistakes are irreversible; evidence can only come from costly failures
- **Example:** "If I get this wrong, I can't undo it"
- **Action:** Build knowledge on safer versions first, then scale up

## Summary: The Learning Half of the Loop

**Summary:** Learning closes the feedback loop by turning individual executions into lasting patterns. Seven disciplines make the loop work: capture evidence immediately, review weekly, promote slowly with explicit criteria, update confidence tiers honestly, delete false patterns, actively search for contrary evidence, and escalate when stuck. The system compounds because each cycle refines your Knowledge Base and makes the next cycle more efficient. You don't need expertise upfront—you need speed, honesty, and discipline.

### **Seven Core Disciplines**

1. **Capture evidence** — what you expected, what happened, what surprised you
2. **Review evidence** — does it confirm or break existing patterns?
3. **Promote slowly** — repetition + variation + consistency required before tier change
4. **Update tiers** — confidence changes as evidence accumulates
5. **Delete false patterns** — don't keep beliefs that evidence disproved
6. **Watch for bias** — confirmation bias, absent evidence, correlation confusions
7. **Escalate when stuck** — if evidence doesn't improve, ask for expert help

### **Why the System Scales**

| Element | Effect |
|---|---|
| More evidence | Better patterns |
| Better patterns | Better decisions |
| Better decisions | Better execution |
| Better execution | Faster learning |
| Faster learning | Stronger next cycle |

**Each cycle is stronger than the last. You don't need to be smart upfront. You need to learn fast and honestly.**

## FAQ: Learning Questions

**Q: How often should I review and update my Knowledge Base?**
A: After every significant execution (weekly minimum). Look for new patterns, tier changes, and contradictions. If you're executing frequently, promotions from GUESSED to KNOWN should happen every 1-2 weeks.

**Q: What if I capture evidence but evidence doesn't point to a pattern?**
A: That's normal. Keep capturing. Real patterns require 5-10 instances before they emerge. Premature pattern detection is just confirmation bias. Wait for repetition.

**Q: Should I write down evidence immediately, or later when I have time?**
A: Immediately, within 24 hours. Memory fades. Details become fuzzy. Capture while context is fresh. A rough note now is better than a perfect memory later.

**Q: How do I know if my tier promotion is honest?**
A: Use explicit criteria before promoting (e.g., "I promote to KNOWN only if it works across 3 different contexts"). If you skip the criteria, you're probably overfitting. Slow promotion prevents that.

**Q: What if contradictory evidence appears after I've marked something KNOWN?**
A: Don't panic. Mark the contradiction in your Knowledge Base. You don't abandon the pattern (one exception ≠ reversal). Refine the boundary: "Works 90% in context X, 10% in context Y."

**Q: Can I learn from watching someone else, or only from my own evidence?**
A: You can learn from others, but mark it lower confidence (GUESSED or INFERRED instead of KNOWN) until you reproduce it yourself. Their context is different. Validate in your specific domain.

**Q: How many failures is acceptable before escalating?**
A: If your tier is stuck at GUESSED after 10+ attempts, escalate. If every approach fails consistently, escalate sooner (3-5 attempts). Stakes determine the threshold.
