# Cognitive Operating System — The Closed Feedback Loop

## Quick Answer

**The COS is a framework for operating safely at the edge of your knowledge base in any domain.** It combines closed feedback loops (knowledge informs action, action produces evidence, evidence refines knowledge) with three operating rules: act small, measure fast, trust slowly. You don't need domain expertise first. You need structured learning while executing.

---

## The Closed Feedback Loop

**Summary:** The core system is a cycle where knowledge guides action, action produces evidence, and evidence improves knowledge. Each cycle compounds. Better knowledge leads to better decisions. Better decisions lead to better outcomes. Better outcomes generate stronger evidence. This compounding is why you can operate in domains you're not yet expert in.

```
Knowledge Base (what you know)
    ↓ informs
Execution (what you do)
    ↓ produces
Evidence (outcomes and surprises)
    ↓ updates
Knowledge Base (loop repeats, stronger each cycle)
```

**Why this matters:** Most of any domain is not knowing things. It is managing risk at the knowledge boundary and expanding that boundary through feedback. You cannot eliminate unknown unknowns. But you can operate safely while expanding what you know faster than you can fail.

### **01-Knowledge-Base.md**

**Summary:** The Knowledge Base is the first side of the loop. Use the Five Pillars (Ontology, Dynamics, Epistemology, Teleology, Control) to structure domain knowledge. Build it as you execute in three phases: baseline primitives, gather evidence, promote patterns slowly. Never wait for complete knowledge before starting.

**Covers:**
- The Five Pillars — primitives, state transitions, observable symptoms, goal-relative importance, mitigations
- Controlled vocabularies and stable naming conventions
- Evidence tiers separating guessed from known
- Building Knowledge Base as you execute (baseline → gather evidence → promote patterns)
- Validators that detect contradictions and refine boundaries

**Start here if:** You're entering a new domain and need to organize what you know (or don't know) about it.

### **02-Execution.md**

**Summary:** Execution is the second side of the loop. Transform a goal into an actionable spec. Apply the 6-Factor Risk Model to decide if an action is safe to try autonomously or needs escalation. Capture evidence immediately so the feedback loop can refine your knowledge.

**Covers:**
- From goal to spec (input state → desired output state → acceptance criteria)
- The execution principle: act small, measure fast, trust slowly
- The 6-Factor Risk Model (blast radius, reversibility, observability, novelty, privilege, time pressure)
- Decision rules for autonomous execution, scope reduction, and escalation
- Evidence capture patterns for the feedback loop

**Start here if:** You're about to act on a goal and need to know: is this safe to try? Should I escalate? How do I capture what I learn?

### **03-Learning.md**

**Summary:** Learning closes the feedback loop. After each execution, process evidence to refine your Knowledge Base. Distinguish what you observed from what you inferred. Promote patterns to "known" only after repetition. Mark confidence honestly so you don't confuse guesses with facts.

**Covers:**
- What counts as evidence (observation + context + outcome)
- Five questions to ask after execution
- Promoting evidence to knowledge (repetition, survival under variation, consistency)
- Confidence tiers and promotion rules
- The learning discipline (capture immediately, review weekly, promote slowly, delete when disproven)
- Failure modes: confirmation bias, absence-of-evidence trap, correlation-causation confusion

**Start here if:** You just finished executing and want to know: what did I learn? How does this change my Knowledge Base? What should I do differently next time?

## How to Use This System

**Summary:** Entry point depends on where you are in the feedback loop. If entering a new domain, start with Knowledge-Base (establish baseline) then Execution (identify what you need to learn). If operating in a domain you already know partially, move between Execution (before action) and Learning (after action). If things break, use Foundations to find the right level of analysis.

**Entering a new domain (you have a goal but no knowledge):**
1. Read 01 (Knowledge-Base) — define baseline primitives and failure modes
2. Read 02 (Execution) — transform your goal into a spec and identify what you need to learn
3. Execute and capture evidence
4. Read 03 (Learning) — promote evidence into your Knowledge Base

**Operating in a domain you're learning:**
1. Before action: Read 02 (Execution) — apply the 6-Factor Risk Model to decide if this is safe to try
2. Execute and capture evidence immediately
3. After action: Read 03 (Learning) — process the evidence and update your Knowledge Base

**Debugging when things break:**
1. Read 04 (Foundations) — understand the Five Pillars and find the right level of analysis
2. Read 01 (Knowledge-Base) — is this a primitive failure, or a symptom of something deeper?
3. Read 03 (Learning) — what evidence disproved your assumption?
4. Update your approach and re-execute

## Three Core Operating Rules

**Summary:** COS rests on three rules. First: act small and measure fast instead of waiting for complete knowledge. Second: mark what you know versus guess so you stay calibrated. Third: always close the feedback loop so execution leads to learning, and learning guides the next action.

**Rule 1: Act small, measure fast, trust slowly.**

Don't wait to understand everything. Take reversible actions that produce immediate feedback. Only promote lessons after repetition.

**Rule 2: Make uncertainty first-class.**

Mark what you know, infer, guess, and lack. Don't pretend. This prevents false confidence and keeps you calibrated.

**Rule 3: Close the feedback loop.**

Execution is only half the cycle. Evidence matters only if you capture it. Knowledge matters only if it guides next action. Always complete the loop.

## The Compounding Effect

**Summary:** COS delivers compounding returns. In month 1, you're 20% prepared but can operate safely. By month 3, you're 35% prepared with much faster decision-making. By month 6, you're 50% prepared but operate like someone with 5 years of experience. You don't become expert through years of studying. You become effective through structured learning while executing.

| Timeframe | Knowledge | Decision Speed | Pattern Recognition | Result |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|--------|
| Month 1 | 20% prepared | Slow, conservative | No patterns yet | Can operate safely |
| Month 3 | 35% prepared | Much faster | See some patterns | Escalation triggers accurate |
| Month 6 | 50% prepared | Instant pattern matching | Recognize instantly | Operate like 5-year veteran |

**Key insight:** Each action teaches you something. Each lesson constrains future action. Knowledge compounds. You don't wait to understand everything before starting. You learn while doing.

## When This Works Best

**Summary:** COS works when feedback is fast, mistakes are reversible, and you're willing to mark uncertainty honestly. It breaks when mistakes are irreversible, feedback is slow, or you pretend to know things you don't. Choose this system when the domain allows learning through experimentation.

**Works when:**
- Baseline knowledge exists (enough not to be completely lost)
- Feedback is fast (days, not months)
- Mistakes are reversible (you can learn cheaply)
- You're willing to be explicit (mark what you know, don't pretend)

**Breaks when:**
- Mistakes are irreversible (life-or-death, massive costs)
- Feedback is slow (years to see results)
- You're not honest (pretending to know more than you do)

## The Core Principle

**Summary:** You cannot eliminate unknown unknowns. That is impossible. But you can operate safely at the knowledge boundary while expanding it faster than you can fail. The system does this by making uncertainty first-class, detecting boundary conditions, escalating gracefully, acting small while measuring fast, and compounding learning. You operate effectively not by knowing more, but by learning faster while controlling the risk of being wrong.

**How COS operates safely at the boundary:**

1. **Making uncertainty first-class** — mark what you know, infer, guess, and lack
2. **Detecting boundary conditions** — know when you're extrapolating beyond prior examples
3. **Escalating gracefully** — when outside the boundary, shift from optimizing to gathering evidence
4. **Acting small, measuring fast, trusting slowly** — take reversible actions, get feedback immediately, promote knowledge only after repetition
5. **Compounding learning** — each action becomes a learning event, each learning event constrains future action

**The outcome:** You can operate in knowledge domains you're not yet expert in because you're not pretending to know things. You're learning them operationally while staying inside your safety envelope. This is what makes the system powerful: learning faster while controlling the risk of being wrong.
